Strategic context

Two company goals, one product lever

In H2 2024, Teya had two critical targets falling short. CR response times were above the 5-second pickup goal — not because of headcount, but because terminal-related tickets were accumulating faster than agents could resolve them. And TPV per merchant was below forecast, despite merchant acquisition being on track, pointing to a need to attract higher-ticket business types.

The Terminal team was explicitly tasked with reducing CR burden by addressing root causes: product usability issues and low feature discoverability. This was not a cosmetic refresh — it was a direct response to company-level pressure.

The terminal software had reached functional maturity. Core capabilities were in place. But the product still lacked a consistently great user experience — and a growing backlog of small, impactful UX issues was making the product feel less polished than it was. Merchants weren't discovering features they already had.

The problem

PAX and Sunmi owned the terminal experience. Teya didn't.

Before the redesign, the terminal was a Teya payment app running on top of a generic Android environment controlled by the hardware manufacturer. Merchants who received a terminal saw PAX or Sunmi's OS — their home screens, their navigation, their settings language. Teya existed only inside the app.

This created three compounding problems:

No brand continuity
The terminal idle screen was flat grey Android — no Teya identity, no merchant name, no brand differentiation. The device looked like a generic tool, not a Teya product.
Raw OS exposed to merchants
The launcher menu showed Android-native options: "Make default app (auto start)", "Bring to front", "Open app". This was the hardware vendor's OS talking directly to merchants.
Setup drove support volume
Activation questions were the single largest ticket category — nearly a quarter of all terminal CR contacts. PIN resets alone generated 60–80 tickets per month. There was no self-service path for either.
Before — plain white launcher, no brand ownership
After — Teya TOM launcher
The terminal idle screen: before (hardware vendor's Android OS) and after (Teya's custom launcher, TOM). Same device. Completely different brand experience.

The opportunity: the Sunmi P2 PRO rollout (part of the Terminal Turnaround programme) was creating a new fleet of devices that could ship with a fully owned experience from day one. V3.8 of the terminal software, released 30 August 2024, was the vehicle.

My role

PM on the terminal experience, end to end

I was the product lead for the terminal software redesign — owning the roadmap, prioritisation, and product decisions across all flows. Work began in May 2024 and shipped to GA across 7 markets by September 2024. This was a collaborative project with a design lead who was a true partner on execution; the decisions and direction were mine, the visual craft was built together.

Discovery — how decisions were made

Data first, then interviews, then build

Every major product decision in this redesign had a two-source foundation: a quantitative signal from app data or support ticket analysis, validated by merchant interviews. The calculator is the clearest example — but the pattern ran across all features.

📊
Calculator — from app download data
App download data showed merchants were installing the native calculator app on their Teya terminals. This was the quantitative signal — merchants were leaving the payment app mid-flow. User interviews confirmed it: they were calculating totals before coming back to enter the amount. The built-in calculator addressed this directly. Post-launch: 6,669 terminals used it in a single week (12% of all active terminals), with 97 merchants calculating more times than they charged.
🎤
Pay by Link restructure — from merchant behaviour analysis
Support contacts and merchant interviews revealed a hidden cost: merchants who had Pay by Link enabled faced a mandatory payment method selection screen on every single transaction, even when paying by card. Most used links sporadically. The friction was invisible until we looked. Solution: remove the selection screen from the core flow entirely, give Pay by Link a dedicated section and home screen shortcut.
📊
Sales Overview — from settlement anxiety and feature request volume
The Summaries feature was generating 200–300 support tickets per month, and a consistent stream of feature requests for monthly reporting. Investigation revealed the root cause: merchants believed closing the summary triggered their settlement. They were performing a ritual based on a misunderstanding. The redesign had to deliver better reporting capability while simultaneously reassuring merchants that settlement happens automatically — no action needed.
🎤
PIN reset self-serve — from ticket taxonomy
PIN reset tickets were a discrete, countable problem: 60–80 per month, almost all solvable with self-service. The existing flow required a support call. A QR code recovery path via the Teya mobile app made this a zero-contact resolution for the vast majority of cases.
Key product decisions

Three decisions that shaped the experience

Decision 1 — Sales Overview: preserve the ritual, expand the capability

The core tension: merchants who used Summaries for end-of-day closure had a deeply habitual workflow. Changing it risked destroying trust. But the feature needed to become a real reporting tool — flexible time periods, multi-terminal views, card scheme breakdowns.

The resolution was a naming and architecture decision: "Save to History" replaced "Close Summary". Same ritual, new language that works for both closure merchants and reporting merchants. The default starting time and automatic daily save features let the system honour the closure habit without requiring manual action. The launch email — "Goodbye Summaries, Hello Sales Overview" — was paired with explicit messaging that settlement happens automatically, no daily closure needed.

Decision 2 — Pay by Link: IA surgery over feature flags

The product brief captured the problem precisely: merchants who had Pay by Link enabled but wanted to do a normal card-present payment had to press "card" for every single transaction. The feature flag was taxing the majority use case to serve the minority one.

The temptation would have been to add a setting: "Show payment method selection screen: yes/no". Instead, the decision was to fix the underlying information architecture. Card payment is the default and primary flow. Pay by Link is a distinct workflow that deserves its own home — a dedicated section in the app and a home screen shortcut for merchants who use it regularly.

This benefited all merchants: card payments became faster (no selection screen), and Pay by Link became more discoverable (not buried in a transactional moment where merchants were already committed to card).

Decision 3 — Notifications: build a new channel, not another email

Feature launches had relied on email for merchant communication. The Sales Overview launch email was well-crafted and localised across 7 languages — but email reach has a ceiling. Merchants who missed it continued using Summaries without understanding the changes, generating ongoing support contacts.

The "What's New" in-terminal notification system was my initiative. A persistent badge on the home screen. Full-screen feature cards with branching logic based on merchant configuration (tips on vs. off leads to different onboarding paths). The badge persists until reviewed. Post-launch: 68% of merchants who received a notification opened it at least once within 60 days.

Product craft

The details that merchants remembered

Several moments in this redesign went beyond functional requirements — they were crafted to create genuine delight. All built in partnership with the design lead.

The swipe-to-print easter egg My concept
At the receipt preview screen — the moment after payment approval — merchants can drag the receipt image downward toward the printer. The receipt prints line by line in real time as they swipe. The gesture is contextually obvious in hindsight (receipt on screen, printer below), invisible until discovered, and requires no instruction. It was cited spontaneously by merchants as their favourite feature. It was intentional enough to appear in the external launch communication: "be sure to not miss the receipt (going from virtual to physical!)."
Figma prototype: tips flow + receipt animation
Prototype walkthrough showing the new tips selection flow and the swipe-to-print receipt animation.
QR code scan animation My concept
The activation flow required merchants to scan a QR code — an unfamiliar gesture for less tech-savvy users. Instead of a static instruction screen, we built an animation showing a phone scanning the code. The animated demonstration removed the need for explanatory text and met users where they were, reducing activation confusion without adding steps.
Confetti on activation complete My concept
The activation flow — made more complex by PCI compliance requirements for third-party delivery — needed to feel like an achievement, not an ordeal. A confetti animation on the "Activation complete" screen turned a compliance-heavy process into a moment of genuine celebration. The terminal is ready. Something good just happened.
Activation flow — animated walkthrough
The guided activation flow in motion — including the QR code scan animation and confetti completion moment.
The Teya yellow wave on the home screen
The TOM launcher home screen replaced flat grey Android with the Teya brand illustration — the yellow geometric wave rising from the bottom, consistent with the splash screens, the approved payment screen, and the activation flow. For the first time, the terminal looked like a Teya product when it was idle. The merchant's business name displayed prominently. The status bar read "Teya". The entire device, not just the app, became owned brand territory.
Full-bleed yellow approved screen
The payment approved state went from a black-and-white confirmation to a full-screen Teya yellow moment with a checkmark. Every successful transaction became a brand touchpoint — visible to the merchant and their customer simultaneously. The most repeated moment in the entire product (multiple times per day for active merchants) became the strongest brand impression.

The terminal software redesign shipped as part of Teya's wider Q3 2024 product launch, which also included the Sunmi P2 PRO hardware and Teya Tap (a separate workstream).

Learnings

What this project taught me

Habits are features too. The Sales Overview project was as much about ritual preservation as capability expansion. Merchants weren't just using Summaries wrong — they had built their daily operations around it. The product decision that unlocked adoption was recognising that "Save to History" wasn't a rename, it was a trust signal. We were telling merchants: your ritual still works here.
Delight compounds over repetitions. The swipe-to-print easter egg works because payment approval happens multiple times per day. An animation that delights on transaction 1 still delights on transaction 500 if it's tied to the physical world (the receipt appearing in their hand). Abstract animations fatigue. Physical metaphors don't.
Feature discoverability is a product problem, not a comms problem. The "What's New" notification system was born from watching the Sales Overview launch email fail to reach the merchants who most needed it. Email open rates have a ceiling. A badge on the device they use every day does not. Building a channel is harder than writing an email, but the compounding returns on every subsequent feature launch justified the investment.
IA surgery beats feature flags. The Pay by Link decision could have been solved with a settings toggle. Instead, fixing the information architecture — card is default, Pay by Link has its own home — improved the experience for all merchants, not just those who used links. The right abstraction eliminates the edge case entirely.
Two data sources is the minimum. Every significant product decision in this project had both a quantitative signal and a qualitative validation. The calculator had download data and user interviews. Pay by Link had behaviour analysis and merchant conversations. Data tells you what is happening; interviews tell you why. Neither is sufficient without the other.
