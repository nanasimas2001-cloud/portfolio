Strategic context

Tipping was a lever for a bigger problem: TPV per merchant was below forecast.

In H2 2025, Teya was on track for merchant acquisition volume but average transaction volume per merchant was lower than forecast. The company needed to increase revenue per transaction — not just add more merchants. Tipping was one of several levers available to the Terminal team.

The two flows

Old flow vs. new flow — the core UX hypothesis.

The fundamental difference between the two flows was the decision architecture: one required customers to actively opt out of tipping; the other let them pay without engaging with the tip UI at all.

New flow — tip on payment screen
Tip buttons appear on the same screen as payment. Customer can tap their card without interacting with the tip options at all — passive opt-out.
Old flow — separate tip screen
A dedicated "Add a Tip?" screen appears before payment. Customer must scroll to and select "No tip" to proceed — active opt-out.

Analytics confirmed the hypothesis: terminals with the new tips flow (separate screen, active opt-out) had more tip events on average than the old flow. The old flow — despite requiring customers to make an active choice about tipping — was actually the one where customers were more likely to simply tap through without engaging.

What we tested

Three variables. Segmented by market, MCC, and transaction value.

Key finding
Screen placement
Tip on a separate dedicated screen (customer must actively select "No tip") vs. tip options on the payment screen (customer can tap card to skip). The separate screen — despite appearing to add friction — significantly outperformed because it created an intentional tip decision moment.
Key finding
Button order & value display
Higher percentages shown first (20%, 15%, 10%) vs. lower first. Numerical value shown alongside percentage (+£20, +£15, +£10) vs. percentage only. Higher-first anchoring increased average tip amount — particularly effective in hospitality and food service segments.
Key finding
MCC & market segmentation
Configurations were tested across market and merchant category — what worked in UK hospitality didn't translate directly to Czech food retail. Final output was market-specific and category-specific configurations across all 9 Teya markets.
Product craft

One small detail merchants didn't ask for — and talk about unprompted.

✦
Tip animation. I introduced an animation that plays after a customer adds a tip — a small celebratory moment on the screen. It wasn't tested or A/B'd. It was a product instinct about how the transaction should feel. Merchants cite it unprompted as one of their favourite things about the terminal. It costs nothing in engineering time and adds something that no spec document could have required.

TPV grew faster than transaction volume (+113.6% vs +100.5%), meaning the programme didn't just increase tip frequency — it increased average tip value. A consequence of the higher-first anchoring and value display optimisations. This is the signal that separates a dark pattern (more friction) from a good product decision (better value presentation).

My role

Self-identified opportunity. End-to-end ownership.

This programme was not assigned. I identified the commercial opportunity independently — recognising that the tip flow was underperforming relative to its potential — and designed the testing programme from scratch. I owned hypothesis design, variant definition, market and MCC segmentation strategy, analysis using Mixpanel and SQL, iteration to the final configuration, and rollout across all markets.

I also built the Snowflake dashboard used to track tip performance, and introduced the tip animation — a product craft decision made outside the test framework because I believed it was the right experience.

At the time of writing, the new tips flow (separate screen, active opt-out) is still rolling out — the majority of terminals remain on the old flow. The full impact of the programme will compound as rollout completes.

What I learned
The most impactful variable was screen placement — not the prettiest insight. Forced opt-out (separate screen requiring "No tip" selection) consistently outperformed passive opt-out (tap card to skip). Small friction in the right direction matters more than frictionless UX.
Segmentation was essential. A configuration that maximised tips in UK hospitality actively hurt conversion in low-value grocery transactions. One global default would have left significant TPV on the table — and potentially frustrated merchants in the wrong segments.
TPV growing faster than volume was the most important signal. It confirmed we hadn't just added friction — we'd found the right way to present value. The difference between a dark pattern and a good product decision is whether the customer is better informed, not just more pressured.
The animation wasn't in any brief. Good product work is partly about the things you add because they're right — not because they were specified. Merchants notice.
