The problem

~800 merchants in the UK were using MOTO with other providers. Teya didn't support it.

MOTO (Mail Order / Telephone Order) allows merchants to manually enter card details — essential for hotels taking phone bookings, medical providers handling remote orders, and B2B suppliers. It's also one of the highest-risk payment categories: card-not-present, no physical verification, elevated chargeback exposure, specific PCI requirements.

The UK operations team was behind on revenue targets. Approximately 800 merchants were using MOTO with other acquirers and could switch to Teya immediately — if we supported it. The deadline was October 31st. There was no formal risk team. I had zero prior domain knowledge.

My role

Product owner — end to end, from zero.

I owned the full lifecycle: researching PCI requirements for MOTO, formulating eligibility criteria independently, collaborating with the fraud team on alerts and blocks, designing the onboarding flow, building the CDD questionnaire using low-code tools, and coordinating delivery across engineering, compliance, customer monitoring, and sales.

A critical part of hitting the October 31st deadline was securing sponsorship from the CPO and CTO early. MOTO required multiple teams — engineering, compliance, fraud, customer monitoring — to prioritise work they hadn't planned for. Without executive sponsorship, those teams had no reason to deprioritise their own roadmaps. I identified this dependency early, made the case to leadership, and used their backing to unlock cross-functional alignment that would otherwise have taken months to negotiate.

Key decision 1 — onboarding flow

Early opt-in vs. post-onboarding activation.

Two options for when and how merchants could request MOTO.

Option B — post-onboarding
MOTO visible only after full KYC. Cleaner UX, full data for eligibility — but invisible during the sales process and misaligned with enterprise merchant expectations.
Selected
Option A — early opt-in
Merchant requests MOTO at sign-up. Feature created in "pending" state, activated only after KYC + eligibility pass. Faster to deliver, better fit for sales-assisted enterprise accounts.

Option A reused existing onboarding infrastructure, met the October 31st deadline, and aligned with the high-touch sales process typical for MOTO merchants. MOTO remained inactive until all compliance checks passed.

Key decision 2 — eligibility architecture

Build for MOTO specifically, or start a shared eligibility engine?

Three architectural options were considered.

Selected
Option A
MOTO-specific service in ATA. Fastest to deliver, fewest dependencies, full team control.
Option B
Extend Pricing Engine. No new infra, integrated with offer logic — but blurs pricing and eligibility concerns.
Option C
Shared eligibility engine. Long-term ideal — too slow for the October deadline, requires broad cross-team alignment.

Option A unblocked MVP delivery. The decision log explicitly flagged Option C as the right long-term direction — a conscious tactical choice, not an oversight. The team that came next had context rather than questions.

What I learned

Three honest reflections.

The hardest part wasn't the technology — it was building credibility with the fraud team when I had no prior risk experience. Coming in with a draft eligibility framework, even an imperfect one, opened the conversation far better than coming in with a blank page.
Documenting the Option A vs B trade-off formally — even though the decision felt obvious — created alignment across teams that would have taken weeks of meetings otherwise. Written decisions scale. Verbal decisions don't.
Choosing a tactical eligibility service over the architecturally ideal shared engine was the right call for the deadline. But flagging it as technical debt explicitly meant the team that came after had context rather than questions.
The most underrated delivery skill on this project was securing CPO and CTO sponsorship early. Technical problems are solvable — organisational prioritisation is harder. Once I had executive backing, cross-functional teams had a reason to move. Without it, the October 31st deadline would have been impossible regardless of how good the product decisions were.
