The problem

100,000 terminals. Wrong brand. $17.5m sitting idle.

In 2021 Teya (then SaltPay) purchased 100,000 Sunmi P2 PRO terminals at approximately $175 per device — a $17.5m hardware investment. On 4 April 2023, Teya rebranded entirely. Overnight, the entire stock was branded SaltPay — purple covers, old logo — and couldn't be shipped to merchants.

Only ~60 had been deployed by the time I took ownership of the project. With PCI certification set to expire in April 2026, the commercial pressure was real: $17.5m of inventory sitting idle, a company brand relaunch that needed a flagship product moment, and a supply chain and operations team unable to scale without a faster setup process. The window to fix all three simultaneously was the launch target of September 2024.

Three compounding problems

It wasn't one problem. It was three at once.

Wrong brand on 100k devices
All terminals had purple SaltPay printer covers. The Teya brand was lime green (Pantone 388C). New injection-moulded covers had to be designed, manufactured, and fitted before a single device could ship.
Neglected supplier relationship
Sunmi had sold Teya 100,000 units but seen almost none deployed. The relationship had gone cold. Remote Key Injection — the scalable path to PCI certification — required active Sunmi collaboration and a new NDA. We needed them engaged and cooperative.
Setup time of 15+ minutes per device
The existing terminal setup flow required sales agents and merchants to configure each device manually — taking 15+ minutes per terminal. At 100,000 units across 7 markets, this was operationally impossible without a faster path.
My role

End-to-end ownership of a cross-functional hardware programme.

I was the Product Manager responsible for the full programme — from supplier relationship management and cover manufacturing, to software development, RKI setup, and the 7-market rollout. The project spanned hardware manufacturing, engineering, supply chain, legal/procurement, sales, customer support, and 7 country operations teams.

01
Rebuilt the Sunmi relationship
Re-engaged Sunmi after a period of inactivity. Negotiated and executed the Remote Key Injection setup — including NDA, transport key generation, physical key components sent internationally by recorded post, and RKI portal configuration. This unlocked scalable PCI-compliant deployment.
02
Solved the rebranding problem
Investigated 5 UK painting and coating suppliers, evaluated durability (Wolff-Wilborn pencil hardness), UV protection, and pad printing vs screen printing. Decision: new injection-moulded covers in Teya lime (Pantone 388C) with pad-printed contactless and Teya logos. Covers sourced directly from Sunmi via Sanmina — our approved warehouse partner — who also handled device preparation and staging at scale.
03
Built the custom Android launcher
Led development of a custom Android launcher for the Sunmi terminal — in partnership with the design lead — including home screen, navigation bar, status bar, and a guided setup flow. This reduced terminal setup time from 15+ minutes to under 3 minutes (5×) and became one of Teya's most recognised brand expressions.
04
Coordinated the 7-market rollout
Managed phased deployment across UK, Portugal, Slovakia, Hungary, Czech Republic, Croatia, and Iceland — coordinating supply chain, sales training, CR training, help centre localisation, and market-specific feature readiness (meal vouchers, EPOS integrations). Tested 4,000 device battery health in-house before shipping.
Key architectural decision — PCI key injection

Three options. One had to scale to 100,000 devices. Only one qualified.

A critical blocker to deploying Sunmi terminals at scale was key injection — the PCI-required process of loading encryption keys onto each device so card data can be securely encrypted at the point of sale. This hadn't been properly solved when the terminals were originally purchased. Three options were evaluated:

Option B
Sunmi Local Key Injection
Keys loaded manually in secure room via Key Loading Device. Available immediately. Not scalable for GA — all terminals would need to be physically shipped to Portugal for key loading at $700/master terminal.
Option C
Teya's own RKI solution
Cheapest option — reuse existing HSM. But not PCI certified, not scalable, and required Teya security approval that wasn't guaranteed. Ruled out immediately for a 100k device deployment.
Selected
Option A
Sunmi Remote Key Injection
Already PCI certified. Fully scalable — no device limit for global availability. Keys loaded remotely from each country. Requires Sunmi collaboration, NDA, and transport key exchange. Only option that supported unlimited global availability.

After selecting Sunmi RKI, a secondary cost optimisation was identified: the initial implementation used a double-key approach, incurring double licensing fees per device. Moving to a single BDK (Base Derivation Key) — still fully PCI compliant — halved the ongoing RKI cost per terminal across the entire 100k device fleet.

14,000 Sunmi devices were deployed in 2024 — the launch year that proved the programme worked. 47,390 followed in 2025, a 3× year-on-year increase. The Sunmi P2 PRO became Teya's primary terminal offering, with the entire 2025 merchant growth strategy built around continuing deployment. The Sunmi rollout also established the operational infrastructure — supply chain pipeline, RKI process, multi-market training framework — that will carry Teya's future terminal generations.

What I learned
The most underestimated part of this project was the supplier relationship. Sunmi had sold us 100,000 devices and seen almost none deployed — from their perspective, we were a neglectful partner. Rebuilding that relationship wasn't a procurement task, it was a trust task. It required showing up consistently, being honest about our timeline pressure, and making them feel invested in our success.
The 5× setup time reduction was the project's most consequential product decision — but it wasn't initially obvious that it was a deployment precondition. I identified early that the deployment maths didn't work at 15 minutes per device and worked backwards from "what does the setup flow need to look like for this to be operationally viable at 100k scale?" That framing unlocked the engineering investment in the launcher.
A rebranding problem that looks like a hardware problem is actually a supply chain problem, a manufacturing problem, a quality assurance problem, and a legal problem simultaneously. I had to learn pad printing, UV coating, plastic composition, and PCI key injection — including evaluating three key injection architectures — in the same project. The breadth was uncomfortable — and exactly what made the outcome possible.
Even after the major decisions were made, there was still optimisation work to do. The initial RKI implementation used a double-key approach that doubled licensing costs per device. Identifying that and moving to single BDK — without any impact on merchants or PCI compliance — was the kind of quiet, high-value work that doesn't make headlines but saves meaningful money at 100k device scale.
This was Teya's first major product launch as a company. The commercial pressure was real — but so was the symbolic importance. Getting it right meant the company had proof it could coordinate a multi-market hardware launch at scale. That credibility had value beyond the terminals themselves.
